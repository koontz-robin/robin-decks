Overview

Introduction to the BEACON

The Rev.io Sales BEACON is your guide to sales success. Selling is both a science and an art — the BEACON maps the science. Your implementation and repetition perfects the art.

Key Principle

Sales is either the highest paid hard work or the lowest paid easy work. Your success depends on your attitude, ability, and work ethic. The choice is yours.

Why Role-Play Matters

Professional athletes practice their basic skills repeatedly before taking the field. Salespeople must do the same. Mistakes in role-play are quickly corrected — the same mistakes with a real prospect cost you money.

  • Role-play with your manager and fellow salespeople regularly
  • Don't wait for scheduled sessions — brief informal role-plays work too
  • Read the BEACON regularly — you'll find new insights each time

Three attributes drive sales success. All three are necessary — but they are not equally weighted.

The Key Attributes

Presentation Skills

People buy from people. Rev.io salespeople make presentations that are compelling, interesting, entertaining, and effective. They help clients visualize benefits and know how to make clients smile, nod, and engage. If the client doesn't buy the salesperson, they won't buy the solution.

Creativity

Never give a "canned" presentation. Find ways to involve the client in the sales process. Pick up on prospect interests and personal values to create a personalized experience.

Competitive Knowledge

Know your competition — both their strengths and weaknesses. Don't disparage competitors. Show how Rev.io provides a better solution for the client's specific needs. Let client references do the separating.

GRIT

Angela Lee Duckworth on Grit

"Perseverance and passion for long-term goals." Grit has a bigger impact on success than IQ or talent. The tenacity to keep fighting through adversity is what separates good from great. Even the best salespeople have down months — putting your ego aside and selling through it is the difference.

Phase 1 — Preparation

Goal Setting

People who set good goals earn more and lead more productive lives. A goal is not a goal until it is committed in writing.

SMART Goals

Specific

"I will increase my income by 20% in the next 60 days" — not "I will increase my income."

Measurable

"I will close XX deals over the next 6 weeks" — not "I will increase my sales."

Attainable

Challenging but achievable. Hitting your annual quota in Q1 is a great ambition — give yourself time to execute.

Realistic

Your goal must be believable to you. If you don't believe it, you won't strive for it.

Time Bound

Goals cannot be open-ended. Attach a realistic timeframe to every goal — short or long term.

Goals vs. Systems (Atomic Habits)

Achieving a goal only changes your life for the moment. Fix the inputs and the outputs will fix themselves. True long-term thinking is goal-less thinking — it's about the cycle of endless refinement. Commit to the process.

Goal Setting Tips

  • Limit short-term goals — focus beats volume
  • Set goals in all areas — professional and personal
  • Set process goals — not just outcomes
  • Write goals in the positive
  • Start now — write a goal before you close this page

The Ramp Trap — Avoid This

Warning

Heavy prospecting → pipeline fills → focus shifts to closing → prospecting drops → pipeline empties → back to heavy prospecting. This cycle of peaks and valleys is the most common mistake new reps make. Prospect every single day, regardless of where you are in your pipeline.

Phase 2 — Prospecting

Outreach & Gatekeeper Scripts

Getting Past the Gatekeeper

Short, sweet answers. Don't volunteer unnecessary information. Say "thanks" after each response to maintain control.

Gatekeeper: "Good morning, ABC Company." You: "Hi… Steve, please." Gatekeeper: "Who should I say is calling?" You: "Bernard. Thank you." Gatekeeper: "What is this in reference to?" You: "Your software platform. Thanks."

6-Step DM Script

  1. Introduce yourself and your company
  2. Reason for the call (separation — show you've done your homework)
  3. Benefit / value statement
  4. Close for the appointment (alternate time close)
  5. Cement the appointment
"Hi [Name], this is [Your Name] with Rev.io. How've you been? I noticed you're leveraging [their platform/tech] as your switch. We're a software solution for [their type of company] and we're currently working with over [X] companies like yours. I know I'm calling out of the blue — let's coordinate a few minutes to walk you through how we're helping [specific outcome]. I have an opening [Day] at [Time 1] or [Time 2] — which works better for you? Great, I'll send a calendar invite right now. What's the best email?"

Cementing Questions

  • What software platforms are you currently using to run your business?
  • How much total revenue do you bill each month?
  • What's your mobile number so I can text a reminder?
  • Who else should be on the call?
Phase 2 — Prospecting

Handling Prospecting Objections

The objection handling cycle: Empathy → "But first…" → Separation → Value Statement → Close for the appointment.

Not the Right Time

"I totally understand — a big software change requires resources. But from my experience, the evaluation process alone takes months, plus 3–6 months to migrate and train. Let's get the conversation started now so you're ready when the time is right."

Send Me Information

"Makes total sense. But without knowing your specific situation, I'd be sending you generic materials that may not be relevant. What's your biggest challenge with your current platform? That way I can send you exactly what matters."

Happy with Competitor

"I totally expected you'd have something in place — everything must be working perfectly or you wouldn't have waited for my call. Help me understand: why would you never be open to an alternative?"

Not Interested

Option 1: "I didn't expect you to be interested — you don't know enough about Rev.io yet. Can I bounce a quick idea off you?" Option 2: "Many people tell me the same thing. But as they learn more about the impact we have on efficiency and revenue, they're usually open to a few minutes."

Phase 3 — Step 1

The Approach

The opening sets the tone for everything that follows. Find the hot button, confirm the DM, and set the agenda before touching product.

4 Parts of the Approach

  1. Thank the prospect for their time
  2. Find the Hot Button — get them relaxed and talking freely
  3. Confirm the DM — "Last time a change was made, you were the one who signed?"
  4. Set the 3-Part Agenda — "Tell you about us → learn about you → if there's a fit, schedule a demo. Does that sound fair?"

Survival Phrase

If prospect says "Just tell me what you've got": "What I'm offering is a comprehensive software solution. But first, I have a few questions to help me understand your business needs."

Confirming the Decision Maker

"Out of respect for your time — is there anyone else involved in the decision-making process? And last time there was a change made, you were the person who signed the paperwork — so you don't need to pass this on to anyone else?"
Phase 3 — Step 2

Company Story

Sell yourself, sell your company, sell your product — in that order. The prospect must like and trust you before they'll trust Rev.io.

Be energetic, excited, and knowledgeable. Weave in what you know about the prospect's business to keep them engaged. Use tie-downs after each key point to confirm engagement.

Tie-Down to End the Story

"Now that you know about our company and our services — do you feel confident that Rev.io can handle your operations needs?"

Transition to Discovery

"Now that you're comfortable with Rev.io, I'd like to find out more about your company and your needs."

Rev.io Value Statements

  • "Using Rev.io as an extension of their team, a client with less than 5 employees grew from $40K to $250K in two years without adding overhead."
  • "Since implementing Rev.io, Spectrotel reduced their operations staff of 8 down to 2 — even while growing from 48 to 80+ employees."
  • "One client eliminated manual operations processes, gained over 1% in revenue, and saved $4K/month by consolidating onto Rev.io."
Phase 3 — Step 3

Discovery & Identifying DBMs

The most important part of the call. The prospect must talk at least 50% of the time from here onward. Your job is to ask, listen, and dig deeper.

Business First Questions

  • What's the background of the company — how did you get started?
  • Who are your competitors and how do you differentiate?
  • What are your top 2–3 initiatives for the next year?
  • How many clients do you have / how much revenue?
  • What's your revenue target for next year?

Finding DBMs (Dominant Buying Motives)

The DBM Formula

Overview → ask general questions → Problem → listen for pain → Effect → ask how this affects the business. Don't stop at the problem. The effect leads you to the DBM.

Effect Probing Questions

  • "How much time does that cost you?"
  • "What does that cost you in dollars?"
  • "What would happen if you don't fix this?"
  • "How do you feel about that?"
  • "Tell me more about that."
  • "Give me an example of when this happened and how it affected the business."

When You're Stuck

"Tell me more about that." "Please give me an example of a time when this situation occurred and how it affected your business." "What would be your idea for a better solution?"
Rule

If the CEO/CTO is not doing at least 50% of the talking from the start of discovery onward, you are pitching — not discovering. The more DBMs you uncover, the more likely you are to close.

Phase 3 — Step 4

Summarize & Make Sick

Reflect the prospect's pain back to them with consequence. Don't just recap — make them feel the cost of inaction.

The Formula

  1. Transition: "Great. Let's review your current situation…"
  2. Summary: Restate the key facts they told you
  3. DBM + Make Sick: Connect each pain to its cost or consequence — then pause
  4. Repeat for each DBM

Example

"Great. Let's review your current situation… You're managing your team across three different systems that don't talk to each other — your ticketing is in one place, your invoicing in another, and nothing syncs automatically. [pause] And you said your team is spending 15–20 hours a month on manual reconciliation just to close the books… And that you've lost two deals this year because prospects wanted to see capabilities your current platform can't support? [pause — let it land]"
Key Principle

The prospect should acknowledge the pain with "Yeah, that's exactly right" or "It's honestly a nightmare." If you don't get that reaction, you haven't made them sick enough yet.

Phase 3 — Steps 5 & 6

Trial Close & Scheduling the Demo

Never leave a discovery call without a demo booked live. "I'll follow up" is not a next step.

Trial Close First

"Based on what you've shared about [specific pain], does it make sense for us to show you exactly how Rev.io handles that?"

Ask for the Demo

"Let's schedule 45 minutes — I want to tailor the demo specifically to [DBM 1] and [DBM 2] you mentioned. Does [day] at [time] work?"

Book It Live

"Let me send you a calendar invite right now before we hang up."

Who Else Belongs on the Demo?

"Who else should be on that call to see this? Anyone who would feel left out if they weren't included?"
Rule

No demo scheduled without the Decision Maker identified and invited. If the DM won't commit to attending — pause and re-qualify the opportunity.

Phase 4 — Partnership

Partnership

Sales success results in clients who refer. Post-sale activities ensure happy long-term clients who become your best prospecting tool.

Post-Sale Activities

  • Complete and submit paperwork accurately and promptly
  • Send a thank-you note within 24 hours
  • Schedule follow-up check-ins at 30, 60, and 90 days
  • Ask for referrals — specifically and by name
  • Identify expansion opportunities within the account
The Referral Ask

The best time to ask for a referral is when a client has just experienced something positive. Be specific: "Who in your network is in a similar situation to where you were 6 months ago?"

Resource

Forecasting Accurately

Remove emotion from forecasting. Use these four signals to determine if an opportunity is real.

SignalWhat to Look For
TimelineHas the DM agreed to a close date and is holding to it?
Buyer ReciprocationAre they responding to calls/emails, showing up on time, returning favors?
DM EngagementIs the decision maker actively involved in the process?
Contract FeedbackHas the DM given feedback on pricing and T&Cs? Feedback = serious buying signal.
War Room Criteria (Stage 3+)

To be a War Room opportunity: Timeline given, initial demo presented, DM involved, pricing reviewed. If any of these are missing — it's Stage 2, not War Room.
