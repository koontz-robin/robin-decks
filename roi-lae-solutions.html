Executive Summary

This is a reopened late-stage evaluation, not a first look.

LAE Solutions reached a near-signature point with Rev.io in December 2025 after reviewing pricing, implementation path, and core PSA workflows. The evaluation paused around one specific blocker: LAE wanted to see a working Portal integration before committing. That integration is now live and was demonstrated on July 8, which reframes the opportunity around whether the now-available integration removes the blocker that paused an otherwise-ready decision.

Current State

LAE's workflow is effective, but too much work happens twice.

The current stack uses separate systems for CRM, quoting, field-service execution, and accounting. That has helped the business keep moving, but it creates repeated handoffs, manual reconciliation, and uncertainty about which platform should be the long-term system of record.

CRM and quoting are split

Customer and opportunity activity begins outside the field-service platform, while accepted proposals have to become operational work somewhere else.

Billing is recreated manually

The initial deposit invoice is generated from the quoting flow, but later project invoices are rebuilt manually in the field-service system and reconciled in accounting.

Project billing is phased

Common project structures include deposit, progress, and final-payment models. Any replacement workflow must support that rhythm without slowing cash collection.

Inventory visibility needs attention

Inventory and truck-stock workflows have been a gap in alternate paths under consideration and should be validated directly by warehouse and operations users.

Sales follow-up can leak

Tasks, follow-ups, and handoffs can fall through cracks when CRM, quoting, and operations do not share a clean workflow.

Monitoring workflows are changing

LAE is moving its central station monitoring platform from UCC to CMS, creating a timely reason to validate how Rev.io can support alarm-monitoring account data.

Recommended Story

Pick up where December left off.

The strongest evaluation path is not a generic feature demo or a cold restart. LAE had already evaluated the PSA direction and paused for Portal readiness. The next proof point should show one accepted proposal becoming a Rev.io quote, moving through approval, creating a job or project, supporting phased invoicing, feeding field execution, and reconciling cleanly with accounting.

What paused the deal

LAE needed the Portal workflow to be real enough to support quote-to-job operations. That blocker was specific, not a broad rejection of Rev.io's PSA direction.

What changes now

Rev.io can be re-evaluated as the operational hub for customer records, quotes, jobs, projects, tickets, time, invoices, field updates, monitoring account data, assets, and reporting.

Decision Criteria

The evaluation should answer five questions.

1. Does the Portal flow actually work?

LAE should see a real accepted proposal move into Rev.io and understand exactly what syncs, what does not, and which status triggers the handoff.

2. Is invoicing better, not lateral?

The new workflow must simplify deposit, progress, final, and service billing rather than forcing the team to recreate invoices in a different place.

3. Can each department validate fit?

Billing, warehouse, operations, and field teams need short role-specific demos instead of one long generalized walkthrough.

4. Does Rev.io replace enough of the stack?

Rev.io should be evaluated against the full operating workflow, not only as a replacement for one current tool.

5. Is the roadmap acceptable?

Open items such as notes, signed PDFs, invoice data, leads, and Zoho integration should be acknowledged and mapped to decision risk.

6. Is this a five-year foundation?

The platform should support LAE's strategic shift toward larger commercial work, recurring inspections, and more disciplined operating visibility.

Evaluation Plan

Recommended next steps before commitment

Step 1Send a concise Portal integration scope showing current sync objects, trigger logic, open gaps, and roadmap caveats.
Step 3Run a live Portal-to-Rev.io demo using an accepted quote scenario and trace the downstream quote, approval, job, project, and invoice path.
Step 4Hold department-specific reviews for billing, warehouse, operations, and field users so the current LAE evaluation owner can validate each team's part of the operating model.
Step 5Convert the directional ROI levers into a quantified business case using LAE's actual invoice volume, proposal count, field labor, warehouse activity, and reporting time.
Step 6If LAE moves forward, plan for 3-4 days to build the customer instance, then a conservative ~60-day go-live timeline with a dedicated solutions analyst and project manager.
Step 7Set implementation expectations around roughly 1 hour per day of LAE participation during a 30-60 day implementation, with data migration primarily from Simpro CSV exports and Rev.io handling cleanup and upload.
Bottom Line

Rev.io wins if it proves the December blocker is gone.

The opportunity is strongest when positioned as a reopened late-stage decision: one specific integration gap paused momentum, and that workflow is now ready to validate. The remaining work is to prove the Portal integration end to end, confirm the finance-ready invoicing path, show role-specific workflows, and be transparent about roadmap items such as CMS, Zoho, leads, notes, signed PDFs, and invoice-level Portal data.

Recommended framing: LAE already got close to choosing Rev.io. The next decision is whether the now-live Portal workflow gives LAE enough confidence to make Rev.io the operating layer connecting sales, quoting, projects, service, field labor, billing, monitoring, and reporting for the next five years.
