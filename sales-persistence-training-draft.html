Do not check in. Create urgency.

The benchmark

Cold meetings are won by reps who stay useful to busy property managers.

Residential property managers are juggling leasing, turns, maintenance, owner communication, and renter complaints. That does not mean the answer is spam. It means reps need a realistic attempt count, a multi-channel plan, and follow-ups tied to problems the property manager already feels.

Cold call lesson

Execution separates average from elite.

Gong's cold-calling research found average reps booked meetings from cold-call conversations at about 4.6%, while top reps booked at about 16.7%. That is not just more activity. It is better targeting, better talk tracks, and better follow-up discipline around leasing risk.

Cold email lesson

Email-only persistence is fragile.

Cold email reply rates often live in the low single digits. Reps should use email as one lane in the cadence, not the whole highway. Phone, LinkedIn, local fraud examples, market activity, proof points, and timing resets all matter.

Hygiene

Core principle

Persistence is not repeating the ask. It is changing the property-management value of the touch.

The easiest way for reps to become background noise is to send follow-ups that exist only because the rep wants a meeting. A property manager needs a reason to care: fewer scam calls, fewer angry renters, cleaner owner conversations, and earlier visibility into high-risk listings.

Pestering

The rep-centered follow-up

"Just checking in."

"Bumping this to the top of your inbox."

"Any update here?"

These messages ask for attention without adding value. They train a busy leasing or operations leader to ignore the rep.

Professional persistence

The property-manager-centered follow-up

"You mentioned copied listings have created renter confusion. I pulled together the three signals teams use to spot fake rentals before they become access issues."

"If Facebook and Craigslist scams are hitting your active listings, the follow-up should be about market risk, renter communication, and which properties keep getting targeted."

Every touch gives the property manager a new reason to respond.

The persistence ladder

Each touch should advance the conversation.

This is the coaching model: reps can be persistent, but they cannot be lazy. The message must evolve.

Touch 1

Targeted reason

Lead with why this property manager, why this role, and why rental fraud or listing trust matters now. Keep the ask small.

"Saw your team manages rentals across multiple markets. Worth comparing how operators catch copied listings before renters get pulled into scams?"
Touch 2

Useful insight

Bring a fraud pattern, market signal, platform trend, or operational observation that makes the outreach worth reading.

"One pattern we see: scammers copy real rental photos, lower the price, then push renters toward deposits or self-showing access before the manager knows it is live."
Touch 3

Pain implication

Connect the problem to renter trust, leasing-team workload, owner confidence, access risk, or brand damage.

"If a scam listing stays up through the weekend, the leasing team inherits confused applicants, angry calls, and sometimes lockbox or access cleanup."
Touch 4

Proof point

Use a real-world fraud scenario, high-risk property example, or platform-specific scam pattern.

"A common example is the same address and images appearing under a different author at a too-good-to-be-true rent. That is exactly the kind of listing Property Shield is built to flag."
Touch 5

Timing reset

Ask about leasing season, portfolio growth, recent fraud events, owner reporting, or whether scams are spiking in specific markets.

"Is rental scam monitoring a current priority, or is the better timing before peak leasing season or your next owner reporting cycle?"
Touch 6+

Clean close or next cycle

Earn the yes, the no, the right contact, or the future trigger. Do not drift into zombie follow-up.

"Should I close the loop, reconnect before peak leasing season, or speak with whoever owns fraud/listing risk for your portfolio?"
Why prospects go dark

Diagnose the silence before sending another touch.

Silence is not one objection. For residential rental property managers, it can mean the rep never made fraud operational, the timing is wrong, the wrong department owns it, or the prospect does not yet believe fake listings are worth a dedicated monitoring layer.

No pain

They see scams as occasional noise.

Next touch should sharpen the impact: renter confusion, angry applicants, bad reviews, owner questions, and leasing team cleanup.

No priority

Leasing fires are louder today.

Next touch should connect fraud visibility to peak leasing season, high-risk properties, market expansion, or recent scam volume.

No authority

The first contact does not own fraud risk.

Next touch should identify whether leasing, marketing, operations, risk/compliance, or executive leadership owns fake-listing response.

No timeline

There is no evaluation window yet.

Next touch should ask about upcoming portfolio growth, owner reporting, platform changes, budget cycle, or leasing season readiness.

No trust

They are not sure monitoring will work.

Next touch should bring scam examples, screenshot evidence, platform workflow, or a lower-risk pilot around active listings.

Real no

The best outcome may be disqualification.

Professional persistence includes clean exits. A documented no beats pretending every ignored property manager is secretly a pipeline opportunity.

Multithreading

One property manager contact is not a deal. It is a single point of failure.

Persistence gets weaker when every touch depends on one leasing or operations contact replying. Multithreading helps reps understand who owns renter trust, who feels the operational pain, who controls budget, and who cares when fake listings create owner or brand risk.

Scenario plays

Match the follow-up to the property-manager signal.

The same message should not go to every silent property manager. The signal tells the rep what kind of value the next touch should contain.

Asked for info

No reply after fraud overview

Likely issue: the pain is real, but not yet tied to owner, renter, or operational impact.

"I may be early here, but usually when rental fraud conversations go quiet it is because the team has not decided who owns the problem. Is this more of a leasing operations issue, a listing/marketing issue, or something leadership would want visibility into?"
Fraud trigger

Recent scam activity

Likely issue: the property manager is reacting to incidents, not yet building a prevention motion.

"If copied listings have already hit your team, it may be worth looking at which properties and platforms are showing the pattern. Want me to send a quick framework for how teams separate one-off scams from repeat high-risk properties?"
Discovery ghost

Pain surfaced, then silence

Likely issue: fraud was acknowledged, but not connected to leasing workload or owner risk.

"You mentioned fake listings have created renter confusion. I do not want to chase a meeting if this is not a priority, but if your team is still fielding scam-related calls, I can send the three areas teams usually inspect first: platform, property, and renter impact."
Demo complete

Next step missing

Likely issue: the rep showed the dashboard but did not anchor the decision to a fraud-response process.

"The demo covered monitoring, removals, high-risk properties, and reporting. The real question is whether fake-listing visibility should become part of your leasing operations process. Should we schedule 20 minutes with leasing and operations to define what a pilot would need to prove?"
Cadence design

Use 8-12 touches, but make them different.

A good cadence is not more email. It is a sequence of channel shifts and message shifts that ties Property Shield to rental fraud, leasing operations, renter trust, and stakeholder-specific concerns.

Day 1

Phone + email

Specific rental fraud reason for outreach, concise operational problem, simple ask.

Day 3

LinkedIn view/connect

Light social touch for leadership, leasing, or operations. No novella. Nobody joined LinkedIn to read a lease addendum.

Day 5

Fraud pattern email

Copied photos, too-low rent, unknown publisher, Facebook/Craigslist/TikTok risk, or repeated high-risk properties.

Day 7

Phone

Call with a different opener tied to renter confusion or leasing-team cleanup, not the same voicemail script.

Day 10

Proof point

Example of a scam listing workflow: find, verify, remove, prioritize, report.

Day 14

Stakeholder ask

Ask whether leasing, operations, marketing/listings, or leadership owns fraud response.

Day 21

Executive angle

Owner trust, renter experience, brand risk, access risk, and whether the issue scales across the portfolio.

Day 28

Clean close

Ask for the no, the better fraud/listing owner, or the right future trigger.

Rep scripts

Useful follow-up beats empty follow-up.

These are building blocks. Reps should customize the problem, market, platform, property type, stakeholder, and timing for the account.

After first touch

Insight follow-up

"One reason I reached out: rental teams often do not know a copied listing is live until a renter calls angry or confused. If this is not on your radar, no issue. If it is, I can send the 3 signals teams usually inspect first."
After discovery

Pain consequence

"You mentioned [fraud/listing pain]. The reason I am following up is that teams usually either build a monitoring process before peak leasing season or keep reacting after renters are already misled. Which side are you closer to right now?"
After demo

Decision path

"The demo seemed most relevant around [renter confusion / copied listings / high-risk properties]. Should our next step be a leasing ops review, a leadership ROI pass, or closing the loop until fraud visibility becomes a priority?"
Breakup

Clean exit

"I do not want to keep throwing follow-ups into the void like a tiny sales boomerang. Should I close this out, or is there a better person who owns rental scam/listing risk for the portfolio?"
Manager guide

Inspect rental-manager relevance, not just activity volume.

A rep can complete ten bad steps and still create zero buyer value. Managers should coach whether the touch connects to a property-management pain, the right stakeholder, the right channel, and the next expected buyer action.

Pipeline review

Questions managers should ask

1What changed between the last touch and this one?
2What rental-manager signal are we responding to: fraud event, leasing season, market growth, owner concern, or scam platform?
3Did the rep add a new fraud insight, property example, proof point, consequence, or stakeholder angle?
4What channel comes next and why?
5What is the clean exit or next-cycle plan if they do not respond?

Training exercise

Make reps rewrite weak follow-ups in real time.

The best part of this training should be practical. Put bad follow-ups on screen and have reps turn them into property-manager-centered touches.

Bad prompt

"Just checking in."

Rewrite it using a rental fraud implication, property example, or leasing-season question.

Bad prompt

"Any update?"

Rewrite it with a stakeholder question: who owns fake listings, renter confusion, or owner reporting?

Bad prompt

"Bumping this."

Rewrite it with new information about scam platforms, copied listing signals, high-risk properties, or proof from the demo flow.

Facilitator close

Persistence standard

Professional persistence means the rep can explain why this touch, why this channel, why this property-management stakeholder, and why now. If they cannot answer those four questions, they are not being persistent. They are just making noise with CRM timestamps.
